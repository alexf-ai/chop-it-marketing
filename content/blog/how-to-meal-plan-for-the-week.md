# How to Meal Plan for the Week (A Simple System That Sticks)

*By Alex Fahey, founder of Chop It. Last updated 16 June 2026.*

**Short answer:** the meal-planning systems people abandon are the elaborate ones — colour-coded spreadsheets, batch-cooking marathons, a month mapped out in advance. The one that sticks is small: pick a handful of dinners for the week, write the one shopping list those meals need, and shop to it once. That's the whole job. Everything else — leftovers, theme nights, prepping ahead — is optional polish on top of those three steps.

This guide gives you a method that survives a normal week, plus how to keep it going past the first enthusiastic Sunday.

---

## Quick answers

- **How do I start meal planning?** Plan dinners only at first, pick 4–5 for the week, list the ingredients, and shop once. Don't try to plan every meal on day one.
- **How many meals should I plan?** Most households need 4–5 dinners a week, not 7 — leave room for leftovers, a takeaway, or a night out.
- **When should I do it?** Whenever you shop. Many people plan Saturday or Sunday before the weekly shop; pick a fixed slot so it becomes routine.
- **What's the biggest mistake?** Over-planning. A rigid 7-day plan breaks the first time life intervenes, and then the whole habit goes.
- **Does it save money?** Yes — planning before you shop is one of the most effective ways to cut both the bill and food waste, since you buy what you'll actually use.
- **Do I need an app?** No, a list works. An app mainly removes the tedious parts: building the shopping list and merging duplicate ingredients across recipes.

---

## Why meal planning is worth the half hour

Two payoffs, both real.

The first is the mental load. "What's for dinner?" is a question most households answer badly every single day, usually at the worst time — 6pm, tired, staring into the fridge. Planning moves that decision to one calm half-hour a week, made once instead of seven times under pressure. The relief of never facing the blank 6pm fridge again is the part people underrate.

The second is money. The average UK family with children throws away around **£60 of food a month — close to £1,000 a year** (WRAP), and the main cause is buying without a plan, then watching it spoil. When every item on your list maps to a specific meal, far less of it dies in the salad drawer. Planning is the cheapest way to spend less on food without eating worse.

## The system: five steps, half an hour

### 1. Check what you already have

Before planning anything, look in the fridge, freezer and cupboards. What's about to go off? What's lurking that you forgot you bought? Build a meal or two around that first — it's free food you've already paid for, and it's the difference between a plan that cuts waste and one that just adds to the shop.

### 2. Pick 4–5 dinners, not 7

This is the step that makes or breaks the habit. You do not need a meal for every night. Plan four or five dinners and leave the rest loose for leftovers, a quick fridge-raid, a takeaway, or eating out. A plan with slack survives a normal week. A rigid seven-day plan breaks the first Tuesday something comes up, and once it breaks people drop the whole thing.

Mix it up so you're not facing five involved recipes: a couple of quick 20-minute dinners, one that makes leftovers for lunch, one old reliable you can cook half-asleep, and maybe one new thing.

### 3. Write the single shopping list

Go through your chosen meals and write down everything they need — then merge the duplicates. If three recipes use onions, that's one line, not three. This consolidation is the fiddly bit people get wrong by hand, and getting it wrong is how you end up with three half-used bunches of coriander.

### 4. Shop once, to the list

Do one shop against the list rather than several top-up runs. Frequent small trips are where impulse buys and duplicate perishables creep in. Buying once, to a plan, is what keeps both the bill and the waste down. Check the list against your step-1 inventory so you're not re-buying what you've got.

### 5. Stay flexible during the week

The plan is a default, not a contract. Swap Tuesday's meal to Thursday if plans change; cook the quick one on the night you're shattered. The point is to remove the daily decision, not to lock yourself into a timetable. A plan you bend is a plan you keep.

## How to actually keep it going

Most people manage one good week and then drift. A few things keep it alive:

- **Keep a shortlist of 10–15 meals you can cook on autopilot.** Planning gets fast when you're choosing from a known set rather than inventing from scratch every week. Rotate one or two new recipes in so it doesn't get stale.
- **Pick a fixed planning slot and attach it to something you already do** — the morning coffee before the weekend shop, say. Habits stick when they're pinned to an existing one.
- **Don't aim for perfect or healthy-every-night.** The plan that lasts includes the comfort food. A week of "ideal" meals you don't fancy gets abandoned faster than a realistic one with a Friday pizza in it.
- **Let some weeks be loose.** A messy week isn't failure. Planning three dinners beats planning none, and beats giving up because you couldn't manage seven.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many meals should I plan for a week?**
For most households, 4–5 dinners. Planning all seven leaves no slack for leftovers, takeaways or changed plans, and an over-tight plan is the most common reason people quit.

**What's the best day to meal plan?**
Whenever you do your main shop — commonly Saturday or Sunday. The key is a fixed, repeatable slot rather than the specific day, so it becomes routine instead of a decision.

**How do I meal plan on a budget?**
Plan around what you already have, lean on cheap staples (pulses, frozen veg, eggs, wholegrains), let one or two meals share ingredients to avoid waste, and shop once to a list. The planning itself is the saving — it stops the impulse buys and spoilage that quietly inflate the bill.

**How do I meal plan for just one or two people?**
Cook recipes that scale down cleanly or deliberately make extra for lunch the next day. Buying for one or two without waste is the real challenge, so favour ingredients you'll use across more than one meal.

**Is meal planning worth it?**
If you find dinner decisions draining or you waste food, yes. It trades a daily 6pm scramble for one calm half-hour a week and cuts the roughly £1,000 a year the average family wastes. If you already enjoy improvising nightly and waste little, you may not need it.

**What if my week never goes to plan?**
Then plan loosely — three or four meals with no fixed nights. Flexibility is a feature, not a failure of planning. A bendable plan you keep beats a perfect one you abandon.

---

## The bottom line

Meal planning works when it's small: a handful of dinners, one merged shopping list, one shop, and the freedom to swap nights around. The systems that fail are the over-engineered ones. Start with four or five meals this week, plan around what's already in the fridge, and shop once — that's enough to feel the difference at the till and at 6pm, and it's a habit you can actually keep.
