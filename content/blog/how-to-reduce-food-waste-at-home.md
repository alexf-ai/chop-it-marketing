# How to Reduce Food Waste at Home (A Practical UK Guide)

*By Alex Fahey, founder of Chop It. Last updated 16 June 2026.*

**Short answer:** the average UK household throws away food worth hundreds of pounds a year, and the single biggest cause isn't laziness — it's shopping without a plan and buying more than the week actually needs. The fixes that move the needle are dull but effective: plan your meals before you shop, buy to that plan, store food so it lasts, and cook from what you already have before it goes off. Get those four right and most of the waste disappears, along with a chunk of the bill.

This guide covers where home food waste actually comes from, the habits that cut it most, and how to make the changes stick.

---

## Quick answers

- **How much food does a UK household waste?** UK households bin roughly **£14 billion** of edible food a year. For a family with children that's about **£60 a month — close to £1,000 a year** (WRAP).
- **Where does most food waste happen?** In our own homes. Around **60% of UK food waste is household waste**, not shops or restaurants.
- **What's the number one cause?** Buying more than you use — shopping without a plan, then watching it spoil. "Not used in time" and "cooked or served too much" are the biggest single reasons.
- **What's the most-wasted food?** Fresh fruit and veg, bread, and milk top the list — the perishable staples people overbuy.
- **What's the single most effective habit?** Planning meals before you shop, then buying only to that plan.
- **Does the freezer help?** Hugely. Most food can be frozen, including bread, milk, cheese and cooked leftovers, and freezing essentially pauses spoilage.

## Why home food waste is a bigger deal than it looks

Food waste feels like a minor household sin — a sad bag of spinach, a heel of bread. Across a year it adds up to real money. UK households throw away about **6.4 million tonnes** of food annually, the large majority of it edible, worth roughly **£14 billion**. WRAP, the government-backed body that runs the Love Food Hate Waste campaign, puts the cost for a typical family with children at around **£60 a month**.

And it's overwhelmingly a *home* problem. It's tempting to blame supermarkets and their wonky-veg policies, but **around 60% of UK food waste happens in households** — well ahead of retail, hospitality or manufacturing. Which is good news, in a way: it's the part you can actually control.

There's a climate angle riding along with the money. Food that's grown, packed, transported and refrigerated only to be binned carries all of that carbon with it for nothing. Cutting household food waste is one of the more effective everyday things a person can do for both their budget and their footprint.

## Where the waste actually comes from

When WRAP digs into *why* food gets binned, the same reasons come up again and again:

- **Not used in time.** Bought with good intentions, forgotten at the back of the fridge, found a week later past saving. This is the big one.
- **Cooked or prepared too much.** Portions bigger than appetites, with leftovers that never get eaten.
- **Personal preference and date confusion.** People bin food on the "best before" date thinking it's unsafe, when best-before is about quality, not safety.

Notice what these have in common: they're planning and habit problems, not cooking-skill problems. You don't waste a courgette because you can't cook it. You waste it because you bought it with no specific meal in mind, and nothing ever called for it.

The most-wasted foods follow the same logic — they're the **perishable staples people habitually overbuy**: fresh fruit and vegetables, bread, and milk. The stuff you grab "to have in," not the stuff you bought for a particular dinner.

## The four habits that cut the most waste

You don't need to compost, ferment or become a different person. Four changes do most of the work.

### 1. Plan the week before you shop

This is the highest-leverage change by a distance. When you decide what you're actually going to eat — even loosely, even just the dinners — your shopping list stops being a wishlist and becomes a parts list. You buy the half-bag of spinach the recipe needs, not the full bag "because it's healthy," and then you use it, because something is waiting for it.

Shopping without a plan is the root cause of most of the "not used in time" waste. Fix the plan and you fix the bin.

### 2. Buy to the plan (and resist the top-up trap)

UK shoppers now make a lot of small, frequent shops rather than one big weekly one. More trips means more impulse buys and more duplicate perishables — a second bag of salad bought because you forgot about the first. Buying once, against a list, beats five top-up runs for keeping waste down. Check the fridge before you go so you're not re-buying what you already have.

### 3. Store food so it actually lasts

A surprising amount of waste is just bad storage:

- **Keep the fridge at or below 5°C.** Many home fridges run warmer than people think, which quietly shortens the life of everything inside.
- **Most bread freezes brilliantly** — slice it first and toast from frozen.
- **Milk, cheese, and most cooked leftovers freeze too.** The freezer is a pause button, not a last resort.
- **Store fruit and veg properly:** most veg lasts longer in the fridge; keep potatoes, onions and bananas out of it.
- **Learn the date labels.** "Use by" is about safety — respect it. "Best before" is about quality — food is usually fine after it, so judge by look and smell.

### 4. Cook from what you've got first

Before you plan anything new, look at what's already in the fridge that's on its way out, and build a meal around it. A "use it up" dinner once or twice a week catches the food that would otherwise be tomorrow's waste. Soups, stir-fries, frittatas, traybakes and curries are forgiving homes for odds and ends.

## Frequently asked questions

**How much money can I save by reducing food waste?**
For a typical UK family with children, wasted food costs around £60 a month — close to £1,000 a year (WRAP). You won't reclaim all of it, but cutting even half of avoidable waste is a meaningful saving for an hour of planning a week.

**Is "best before" the same as "use by"?**
No, and this distinction wastes a lot of food. **Use by** is a safety date — don't eat food after it. **Best before** is a quality date — food is usually perfectly safe afterwards, just possibly past its peak. Judge best-before foods by sight and smell rather than binning on the date.

**What foods can I freeze that people don't realise?**
Bread (slice first), milk, hard cheese (it crumbles after thawing but cooks fine), cooked rice (cool quickly, freeze, reheat until piping hot), most cooked leftovers, herbs (chop into ice-cube trays with a little oil or water), and even eggs out of the shell. The freezer pauses spoilage on almost everything.

**What's the most-wasted food in UK homes?**
Fresh fruit and vegetables, bread, and milk — the perishable staples that get bought "to have in" rather than for a specific meal, then spoil.

**Does meal planning really reduce waste, or is that just a slogan?**
It genuinely does, because most household waste comes from "not used in time" — food bought with no plan for it. When every item on your list maps to a meal, far less of it dies in the salad drawer.

**What should I do with food that's about to go off?**
Cook it tonight. A "use it up" meal — soup, stir-fry, frittata, traybake, curry — absorbs odds and ends. If you can't cook it in time, freeze it; freezing buys you weeks.

## The bottom line

Home food waste isn't a willpower failure or a cooking-skill gap. It's mostly a planning gap: food bought without a specific use, then forgotten until it's too late. The fixes are unglamorous — plan before you shop, buy to the plan, store food well, and cook what's about to turn — but together they cut both the bin and the bill, and they pay back the small effort every single week.

Start with one week of actually planning your dinners before you shop. It's the change that does the most, and you'll feel it at the till and in how little ends up in the caddy.
